# VALIDATION_CHECKLIST

## Purpose
Use this checklist during demos and follow-up conversations with:
- Medicare agency owners
- agency managers
- service and compliance leaders
- people adjacent to senior-market distribution

The goal is to validate:
- whether the problem is real and urgent
- whether the workflows match how agencies actually operate
- whether the compliance-first positioning is credible
- whether the separate follow-up workflow is valuable without blurring Medicare activity
- whether the product is strong enough to justify a pilot or paid engagement

## How To Use This Checklist
- Use it during or right after a product demo.
- Capture answers in plain language, not just scores.
- Ask what they do today before asking whether they like the product.
- Separate feedback from owners, managers, compliance reviewers, and distribution-adjacent contacts because their priorities will differ.
- Pay close attention to where they become uncomfortable, confused, or unexpectedly excited.

## 1. Problem Validation

Checklist:
- Do they agree that admin work around conversations, consent, QA, routing, and follow-up is fragmented today?
- Do they describe current work as manual, slow, or error-prone?
- Do they feel compliance review and documentation create operational drag?
- Do they already have pain around tracking what was said, who handled it, what consent exists, and what should happen next?
- Do they think the problem is painful enough to justify workflow software rather than a lighter reporting tool?

Signals to look for:
- Strong validation:
  - “We live in spreadsheets, notes, and memory.”
  - “This would save my team real time.”
  - “We have too many places where things get lost.”
- Weak validation:
  - “Our CRM already handles this well enough.”
  - “This is not a top-three problem.”

## 2. Workflow Validation

Checklist:
- Does the dashboard feel like a believable operations starting point?
- Does the conversations inbox match how they triage work today?
- Does the conversation detail page surface the right level of summary, flags, transcript evidence, and next actions?
- Does the compliance page reflect how review actually happens?
- Does the consent ledger feel like a useful operational and audit surface?
- Does the tasks page match how work is assigned and tracked?

Signals to look for:
- Which page feels immediately useful?
- Which page feels unrealistic or too demo-like?
- What is missing from the workflow before they could use it daily?

## 3. Compliance Concern Validation

Checklist:
- Do they believe the product is meaningfully different from a sales bot?
- Do they agree the product should not recommend Medicare plans?
- Do they agree the product should not recommend annuities?
- Do they believe the audit trail and consent visibility are sufficient in concept?
- Do they want more explicit controls around reviewer assignment, blocker enforcement, or evidence retention?
- Do they raise legal, carrier, or internal compliance objections to the current flow?

Signals to look for:
- Positive:
  - “I like that it forces review and documentation.”
  - “This feels safer than a generic AI assistant.”
- Risk:
  - “I’m worried users would still treat this like a recommendation engine.”
  - “I need stronger proof that the workflow stays separated.”

## 4. Separate Follow-Up Opportunity Validation

Checklist:
- Do they think identifying separate follow-up opportunities is valuable?
- Do they agree this should remain separate from Medicare workflow?
- Do they believe explicit consent gating is necessary?
- Do they think the opportunity workflow is operationally useful rather than overly cautious?
- Do they see value in tracking signals even when no follow-up happens?

Signals to look for:
- Strong validation:
  - “We miss these opportunities today because they come up informally.”
  - “This separation actually helps us stay cleaner.”
- Weak validation:
  - “We would never use this workflow.”
  - “Our producers already manage this outside the core system and don’t need visibility.”

## 5. Willingness To Pay

Checklist:
- Do they say this solves a problem important enough to budget for?
- Do they frame value in saved time, reduced compliance risk, improved oversight, or better follow-through?
- Would they pay for this as standalone software or only if bundled with something else?
- Do they think pricing should be per agency, per user, per workflow volume, or tied to some other model?
- Do they describe a clear budget owner?

Questions to resolve:
- Who would own the buying decision?
- Would this come from ops budget, compliance budget, or owner discretionary budget?
- Is the likely first sale a pilot, annual subscription, or consulting-led rollout?

## 6. Integration Needs

Checklist:
- What system is their current source of truth for clients?
- Do they need CRM integration to take this seriously?
- Do they need telephony/call recording integration?
- Do they need e-sign, document, or consent-link workflows?
- Do they need calendar or appointment scheduling integration?
- What data would they refuse to enter twice?

Interpretation:
- If they love the workflow but say “not without CRM sync,” that is still strong validation.
- If they say “we would use this manually first,” pilot readiness is higher.

## 7. Pilot Readiness

Checklist:
- Would they take a follow-up call focused on a pilot?
- Would they allow a small team or one office to test it?
- Would they share real workflow examples, sample conversations, or process maps?
- Would they help define pilot success criteria?
- Do they have an internal champion?
- Is there a realistic first use case such as:
  - conversation review
  - consent tracking
  - compliance QA
  - separate follow-up opportunity routing

Signs of strong pilot readiness:
- willing to share real examples
- willing to define success metrics
- willing to identify a pilot owner
- willing to tolerate early product gaps if the core workflow is right

## 8. Red Flags

Watch for:
- They want autonomous Medicare plan recommendation
- They want annuity recommendation from the system
- They want outbound automation that bypasses licensed human review
- They believe compliance separation is unnecessary
- They think the problem is interesting but not painful
- They like the demo but cannot name a real team that would use it
- They insist on many integrations before they can even react to the workflow value
- They say their existing CRM already solves the problem with no meaningful gap

Interpretation:
- Some red flags are disqualifying for the current product direction.
- Others indicate timing or market segment issues rather than product failure.

## 9. Questions To Ask

### Problem Questions
- Walk me through how a Medicare conversation gets reviewed today.
- Where do things get lost between the conversation, consent, compliance review, and next action?
- What part of this process creates the most admin drag for your team?

### Workflow Questions
- Which page in this demo feels most immediately useful?
- Which part of the workflow feels unrealistic or incomplete?
- If you used this tomorrow, what would your team still have to do outside the system?

### Compliance Questions
- What part of this product makes you more comfortable from a compliance perspective?
- What part would make your compliance lead nervous?
- What would you need to see to trust the separation between Medicare work and separate follow-up work?

### Follow-Up Opportunity Questions
- Do separate retirement-income questions surface often enough to matter operationally?
- How do you currently handle those situations?
- Would a separate, consent-gated workflow help or slow your team down?

### Commercial Questions
- If this solved the workflow well, would you pay for it?
- Would you pilot it first?
- Who else would need to approve that decision?

## 10. Simple Scoring Rubric

Use one row per interview or demo participant.

### Core Labels
- `must-have`
- `nice-to-have`
- `not useful`
- `compliance concern`
- `would pay for this`
- `would pilot this`

### Suggested Scoring Table

| Area | Score Options | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Problem urgency | `must-have` / `nice-to-have` / `not useful` | Is the pain real and current? |
| Workflow fit | `must-have` / `nice-to-have` / `not useful` | Does the product match how they operate? |
| Compliance confidence | `must-have` / `nice-to-have` / `compliance concern` | Do they trust the guardrails? |
| Separate follow-up value | `must-have` / `nice-to-have` / `not useful` | Is the separate workflow genuinely useful? |
| Commercial interest | `would pay for this` / `would pilot this` / neither | Do they want to buy or at least test? |

### Quick Summary Scoring
- Strong validation:
  - problem urgency = `must-have`
  - workflow fit = `must-have` or strong `nice-to-have`
  - compliance confidence is not a `compliance concern`
  - separate follow-up value is at least `nice-to-have`
  - they say `would pilot this` or `would pay for this`
- Mixed validation:
  - they like the concept but need integrations or workflow changes first
- Weak validation:
  - problem is low priority
  - workflow fit is weak
  - compliance concern is unresolved
  - no pilot or payment interest
